Our Research Focus Areas

We focus on key areas of theoretical and computational chemistry to address challenges in sustainable energy, chemical synthesis, and materials discovery.

Acid-Catalysed Interfacial Reaction for Cellulose to Biofuel Production

The conversion of cellulose, a major component of biomass, into biofuel is a promising path for sustainable energy production. However, this process faces significant challenges, particularly when conducted in aqueous solutions, which are essential for practical applications to reduce production costs. To address these challenges and improve reaction yields, various strategies have been developed over the years.Â  One particularly effective approach is the use of biphasic media, which combine aqueous and non-aqueous phases. This biphasic system has been found to significantly enhance the efficiency of cellulose-based biofuel production. Our research will focus on investigating the properties of some of the interesting biphasic liquid media, with a specific emphasis on understanding their interfacial structure and its impact on chemical reactions relevant to cellulose-based biofuel production. The studies conducted are expected to provide valuable insights for optimizing the kinetics and selectivity of these reactions in a cost-effective manner. This will be achieved through the manipulation of the interfacial structure using appropriate additives.

Exploring The Mechanism of Formation, Structure, and Properties of Electrode-Electrolyte Interface in Li-ion and Post Li-ion Batteries

The research is dedicated to addressing the critical challenge of energy storage in the face of rising energy consumption and dwindling fossil fuel reserves. Efficient energy storage emerges as a pivotal solution. Li-ion batteries (LIBs) have garnered widespread attention due to their exceptional energy and power densities, making them ideal for applications in portable electronic devices. However, to fully harness their potential in the electric vehicle (EV) industry, significant improvements in energy density, safety, and cost-effectiveness are essential. Consequently, this research is committed to advancing the field by focusing on the development of high-performance electrodes and electrolytes, guided by insights from atomistic simulations and free energy computations. The key objectives include the identification of suitable dopants to enhance the electrochemical performance and stability of existing cathode materials, analyzing the formation, structure, and properties of the electrode-electrolyte interface in Li and post-Li ion batteries to engineer improvements in their electrochemical performance and safety attributes, and developing electrolytes with superior stability and ion diffusion properties through insights from free energy computations and atomistic simulations. It aims to pave the way for more efficient and sustainable energy storage solutions.

Machine Learning-Driven Enhancement of Transition Metal Sulfide Catalyst for Electrochemical Nitrogen Reduction Reaction (eNRR)

The research area primarily focuses on catalysis, particularly in the context of the electrochemical nitrogen reduction reaction (eNRR). A significant facet of this research involves the development of catalysts, with a particular emphasis on transition metal sulphides. The objective is to design catalyst materials that exhibit robust performance in eNRR, ultimately contributing to the sustainable production of ammonia. One key aspect revolves around harnessing the capabilities of machine learning (ML) to guide and inform the rational design of these catalyst materials.Â  Theoretical calculations such as density functional theory (DFT) are employed, providing insights into the intricate chemical processes at play. Ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulations, along with enhanced sampling techniques like metadynamics, are integral components of the research methodology, aiding in the exploration of catalytic activity, reaction kinetics, and thermodynamics. Through an interdisciplinary approach that combines chemistry, materials science, and data-driven techniques, we aim to develop catalysts that are not only highly effective but also environmentally sustainable, thereby contributing to the advancement of eco-friendly ammonia production.
